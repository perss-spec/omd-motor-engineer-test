Senior Motor Design Engineer — Interview

Duration: 45-60 min 25 questions, 5 categories Expected: 5+ years BLDC/PMSM design

A Electromagnetic Theory & Fundamentals

1
Explain the difference between SPM (Surface Permanent Magnet) and IPM (Interior PM) topologies. When would you choose one over the other for a direct-drive propulsion application?
Look for: reluctance torque in IPM, saliency ratio Ld/Lq, field weakening capability, manufacturing complexity. SPM preferred for low-speed direct-drive (simpler, higher torque density at low speed, no reluctance needed).
2
Derive the relationship between KV (RPM/V) and Kt (Nm/A). Why is Ke = Kt in SI units for a sinusoidal PMSM?
Kt = 60/(2π·KV) ≈ 9.55/KV for phase, or ≈ 8.27/KV line-to-line. Energy conservation: P = T·ω = E·I, therefore Kt = Ke.
3
Explain the concept of winding factor in fractional-slot concentrated-wound machines. How do pitch and distribution factors combine, and why do FSCW motors dominate in low-speed high-torque applications?
Kw = Kp × Kd. FSCW: short end-windings → lighter, less Cu loss. High LCM reduces cogging. Trade-off: higher space harmonics → more eddy losses in magnets. Typical Kw = 0.86-0.95 depending on slot/pole.
4
How does cogging torque depend on the LCM of slots and poles? List 3 techniques to minimize it without changing the slot-pole combination.
Higher LCM = lower cogging amplitude. Techniques: magnet arc shaping (bread-loaf), skewing (1 slot pitch), slot opening optimization, dummy slots, chamfered magnet edges.
5
Explain the field weakening region for a PMSM. What determines maximum speed? What is the “characteristic current” and why is it critical for constant-power speed range?
I_char = λ_m / Ld. When I_char = I_rated → infinite CPSR (ideal FW). SPM has limited FW range due to low Ld. Max speed limited by DC bus voltage vs back-EMF.

B FEA & Simulation Methodology

6
How do you model iron losses in a motor operating at high electrical frequency? Compare the classical Steinmetz equation with the Bertotti three-term model.
Steinmetz: P = k·f^a·B^b (empirical, single frequency). Bertotti: P = k_h·f·B² + k_e·f²·B² + k_a·f^1.5·B^1.5 (hysteresis + eddy + anomalous). Bertotti better for wide frequency range. At f > 400 Hz eddy term dominates.
7
When is 3D FEA necessary versus 2D sufficient for motor analysis? Give specific examples where 2D would give misleading results.
3D needed for: axial flux machines, end-winding inductance/losses, short stack lengths (L/D < 0.3), 3D magnet shaping (step-skew), end-effects. 2D overestimates performance by 5-10% for short machines. 2D fine for long machines with simple end-windings.
8
Your 2D FEA predicts the back-EMF constant, but the prototype measures 12% lower. Systematically list all possible causes and how you would diagnose each.
Causes: magnet Br tolerance (−5%), temperature derating, 3D end-effects (2D overestimates), stacking factor error, air gap larger than nominal, magnet assembly shift, wrong BH data. Diagnosis: measure actual Br, check mechanical gaps, thermal measurement.
9
How do you set up multi-objective optimization for a motor design? Walk through variable selection, objective functions, constraints, and algorithm choice.
Variables: tooth width, slot opening, magnet thickness/arc, turns. Objectives: efficiency, weight, cogging. Constraints: Tmax, demagnetization, fill factor. Algorithms: NSGA-II, Bayesian, DOE + surrogate model. Key: define meaningful bounds and constraints first.
10
How do PWM harmonics from the inverter affect motor performance? What design and operational measures help mitigate additional losses?
PWM harmonics → additional iron losses (eddy currents in laminations and magnets), increased Cu AC losses (skin/proximity), audible noise, torque ripple. Mitigation: higher switching frequency, sinusoidal filters, thinner laminations, segmented magnets, Litz wire.

C Manufacturing, Testing & Quality

11
What criteria drive your lamination steel selection? How does operating frequency influence your choice of grade and thickness?
Criteria: saturation (Bsat), core loss at operating f, permeability, cost, availability. At f > 400 Hz: need 0.2-0.35 mm (NO20, B20AV1200). At low f: M19 0.35-0.5 mm sufficient. P_eddy ~ f²·t². Also consider punching/laser cutting effects on loss.
12
Describe your quality control process for motor winding in production. What electrical tests do you perform and what tolerances are acceptable?
Tests: phase resistance (±2%), inductance balance (±5%), hi-pot/surge test (2× rated + 1000 V), insulation resistance (>100 MΩ), back-EMF symmetry. Visual: end-winding clearance, slot fill uniformity. Thermal cycling after potting.
13
You are commissioning a dynamometer test for a newly designed motor. Describe your complete test protocol from mechanical setup to performance characterization.
Setup: alignment, coupling, instrumentation (torque sensor, encoder, thermocouples, power analyzer). Tests: no-load back-EMF vs RPM, locked-rotor torque, R/L, efficiency map, thermal run at rated load, cogging torque. Compare all vs FEA predictions.
14
Compare magnet retention methods for high-speed PM rotors: adhesive-only, Kevlar bandaging, carbon fiber sleeve, and metallic retention ring. Pros/cons of each?
Adhesive: simple, low cost, fails at high temp/RPM. Kevlar: good safety margin, low eddy losses. CF sleeve: strongest, but eddy current losses. Metallic ring: robust but increases effective air gap, adds weight, eddy losses. Choice depends on tip speed and temperature.
15
What IEC and ISO standards are relevant for BLDC motor design, testing, and certification? How do you plan for compliance early in the design process?
IEC 60034 (rotating machines), IEC 60085 (insulation class), IEC 60529 (IP rating), ISO 10816 (vibration), IEC 61800 (VSD). Plan: define insulation class early, thermal model against rating, EMC considerations, documentation trail for certification.

D Thermal Management & Reliability

16
What are the dominant cooling mechanisms in a drone outrunner motor during hover? When would you use an LPTN model versus CFD?
Prop-wash forced convection on bell (h = 20-50 W/m²K). Conduction via stator teeth to housing. LPTN: fast, early design. CFD: detailed, for optimization. Radiation negligible. Key: Cu losses in slots, Fe losses in teeth.
17
Your motor’s winding reaches 155°C instead of the 130°C target at continuous operation. Outer diameter is fixed. What 3 changes would you prioritize and why?
1) Increase fill factor (VPI potting improves thermal conductivity). 2) Better lamination steel (lower iron losses). 3) Optimize winding (fewer turns + higher voltage = lower I²R). 4) Ventilation holes in bell. 5) Upgrade slot liner thermal conductivity.
18
Describe bearing selection criteria for an outrunner motor. How do you calculate L10h life, and what are typical design targets for UAV applications?
L10h = (C/P)^3 · 10&sup6;/(60·n). Factors: radial/axial load, RPM, preload, sealing (2RS for IP55), ABEC class. Typical target: >1000 h at hover RPM. Consider contamination factor for agricultural environment.
19
How would you achieve IP55 protection in an outrunner BLDC motor without excessive weight gain or thermal penalty?
Labyrinth seal at shaft/bell, epoxy potting (VPI), sealed bearings (2RS), bell skirt overlap, baffled vents. Trade-off: sealed = hotter, heavier. VPI helps both thermal conductivity and sealing simultaneously.
20
Excessive vibration is reported in a production batch of motors that individually passed bench testing. Describe your systematic diagnostic approach.
Rotor imbalance (most common), bearing defect, eccentricity, magnet delamination, resonance at operating RPM. Diagnostic: FFT vibration analysis, compare spectrum to healthy unit, check mechanical runout, bearing signature analysis.

E System Integration & Problem Solving

21
Describe a motor project where you faced significant trade-offs between competing requirements. What were the constraints and how did you make the final decision?
Open-ended. Look for: structured decision process, quantitative trade-off analysis (not just intuition), stakeholder alignment, validation through prototype or simulation.
22
How do you validate your FEA model against a physical prototype? What measurements, tolerances, and acceptance criteria do you use?
Compare: back-EMF vs RPM (<5%), locked-rotor torque, phase R and L, thermal run with thermocouples, cogging torque, efficiency on dyno. Acceptable: <5% torque, <3% EMF, <10% thermal prediction.
23
A customer’s motor fleet shows increasing failure rate after 6 months in field. Motors passed all factory tests. Walk through your failure investigation from symptom to root cause.
Data collection: failure mode (open/short circuit, bearing, demagnetization), field conditions. Analysis: disassemble failed units, compare with pristine. Common: insulation breakdown from thermal cycling, bearing contamination, magnet degradation. Corrective action plan.
24
A customer requests 3× reduction in torque ripple without changing the motor’s outer envelope or magnet grade. What approaches would you consider?
Magnet arc shaping (~−50% cogging, ~−1% torque), stator skew 1 slot pitch (~−90% cogging, −3% Kw), slot opening optimization (~−30%, no torque loss), control-based compensation. Combination can achieve >3× reduction.
25
What motor design and simulation tools have you used? Compare two you’re most experienced with — strengths, limitations, and ideal use cases.
MotorCAD: fast multiphysics, template-based, great for design-space exploration. Maxwell: detailed 2D/3D FEA, flexible geometry. JMAG: full optimization loop, production-ready. FEMM: free, good for quick validation. Look for depth of experience, not just tool names.

2 Design Tasks

Task 2.1 — Topology Selection & Justification

Select and justify with quantitative reasoning:

  • Motor topology: outrunner SPM, outrunner IPM, inrunner, or axial flux
  • Slot/pole combination (with winding factor calculation)
  • Winding configuration: concentrated vs distributed, star vs delta
  • Target KV value (derive from thrust/propeller/voltage requirements)

Deliverable: 1-2 page justification with comparison table of ≥2 candidate configurations.

Task 2.2 — Electromagnetic Design

For the selected configuration, calculate or determine through FEA:

  • Magnetic circuit: B_gap, B_tooth, B_yoke flux densities
  • Winding: Turns/coil, wire gauge, parallel strands, fill factor, R_phase, L_d, L_q
  • Performance: Back-EMF waveform (shape + amplitude vs RPM), torque-current curve, cogging torque
  • Losses: Copper losses (hover + max thrust), iron losses (hysteresis + eddy), magnet eddy losses
  • Efficiency: At 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% throttle

Deliverable: FEA screenshots (flux density, back-EMF, cogging plots), calculation files, parameter summary table.

Task 2.3 — Material Selection

Task 2.4 — Thermal Assessment

Provide a thermal estimate for continuous hover operation:

  • Identify all heat sources and magnitudes
  • Estimate winding hot-spot temperature (LPTN or equivalent)
  • Confirm magnet temperature within safe limits
  • Suggest cooling strategy (passive/active, ventilation, potting)

Deliverable: Thermal circuit diagram, temperature estimates, cooling recommendations.

3 Challenge Task

★ Multi-Objective Optimization (Bonus)

Define and execute a multi-objective optimization:

  1. Maximize efficiency at hover (50% throttle)
  2. Minimize cogging torque (% of rated)
  3. Minimize motor weight (active materials)

Design variables (minimum 4):

  • Tooth width, slot opening, magnet thickness, magnet arc ratio, turns/wire gauge

Output: Pareto front, sensitivity analysis, recommended design point with justification.

Tools: Python + PyFEMM, MATLAB + FEA, MotorCAD optimizer, JMAG, or manual DOE.
